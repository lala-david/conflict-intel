# Competitive Analysis: Terrorism & Conflict Intelligence Platforms
**Date:** 2026-03-31

---

## 1. PREMIUM / ENTERPRISE PLATFORMS

---

### 1.1 Janes Terrorism & Insurgency Centre (JTIC)

**What they do:** JTIC aggregates terrorism and insurgency event data across all Janes information sources, monitoring 1,000+ non-state armed groups (NSAGs) globally with analyst-curated incident records.

**Key features:**
- Terrorism event database with charting/analytics builder
- Group profiles for 1,000+ NSAGs
- Geolocation of incidents
- Customizable trend analysis and pattern identification
- Integration with broader Janes defence intelligence ecosystem
- Country risk assessments

**Pricing:** Enterprise subscription; not publicly listed. Janes subscriptions start from ~GBP 666/year for individual magazine products, but JTIC enterprise access is significantly higher (estimated GBP 10,000-50,000+/year depending on modules). Quote-based.

**Target audience:** Military/defence planners, government counter-terrorism agencies, defence contractors, insurance underwriters, academic institutions.

**Data sources:** Open-source media monitoring, Janes analyst network, government reports, field reporting.

**Update frequency:** Daily (new incidents added continuously).

**Strengths:**
- Gold-standard brand recognition in defence/security intelligence
- Deep historical dataset
- Human-verified, structured data with consistent methodology
- Strong integration with broader Janes equipment/military databases

**Weaknesses:**
- Extremely expensive; prohibitive for small orgs or independent researchers
- No free tier or public API
- UI is functional but dated compared to modern SaaS platforms
- Focused on state/military audiences; less accessible for humanitarian or NGO use
- Limited real-time alerting compared to AI-native platforms

**UI/UX:** Professional but utilitarian. Database-style interface with charting tools. Functional rather than modern.

### 1.2 SITE Intelligence Group

**What they do:** Monitors and translates jihadist, white supremacist, and other extremist content online, providing subscribers with near-real-time access to terrorist propaganda, communications, and threat indicators.

**Key features:**
- Real-time monitoring of extremist channels (Telegram, dark web, forums)
- SourceFeed: searchable dataset of hundreds of terabytes from extremist platforms
- SearchFeed: search engine for filtering extremist posts by target, location, actor
- GuideTracker: monitors terrorist training manuals
- Modular services (jihadist, far-right/far-left, cyber, finance, energy/infrastructure)
- Human-verified translations and analysis

**Pricing:** Subscription-based; individual services or bundled packages. Historical pricing ~$2,500/year (2006); current pricing estimated $5,000-25,000+/year depending on modules. Special rates for non-profits. Not publicly listed.

**Target audience:** Intelligence agencies, law enforcement, counter-terrorism units, media organizations, academic researchers.

**Data sources:** Primary-source collection from extremist forums, Telegram channels, dark web, social media, propaganda outlets.

**Update frequency:** Real-time/continuous (24/7 monitoring).

**Strengths:**
- Unmatched depth in extremist content monitoring
- Primary-source access to terrorist communications that no one else provides
- Human analysts providing translation and context
- Trusted by major government agencies and media (often cited by CNN, NYT, etc.)
- Deep historical archive of extremist content

**Weaknesses:**
- Narrow focus: primarily extremist content, not a general conflict/event database
- No geospatial visualization or mapping tools
- Dated web interface; not a modern analytics platform
- No predictive/forecasting capabilities
- Limited quantitative/structured data for trend analysis
- Ethical concerns about platforming extremist content

**UI/UX:** Basic web portal. Text-heavy, email-alert style delivery. No modern dashboards or interactive maps.

### 1.3 Dragonfly TerrorismTracker

**What they do:** Provides a curated, structured database of 70,000+ terrorist incidents and 1,500+ plots globally, with geolocation, categorization, and in-platform analytics.

**Key features:**
- 70,000+ incidents, 1,500+ plots (growing daily)
- 17 category indexes for structured filtering
- Geolocation and mapping (integrates with ArcGIS/ESRI)
- Group profiles and analysis
- Human-researched with quality control (no automated scraping)
- In-platform portal with mapping and analytics
- Terrorism chatter and propaganda monitoring

**Pricing:** License-based; not publicly listed. Available via ESRI ArcGIS Marketplace. Estimated mid-range enterprise pricing (likely $10,000-30,000+/year). Contact for quote.

**Target audience:** Business risk managers, insurance underwriters, government agencies, law enforcement, media, NGOs, universities.

**Data sources:** Open-source analyst research, curated media monitoring, defined methodology with quality control.

**Update frequency:** Daily.

**Strengths:**
- High-quality, human-verified structured data
- Strong ESRI/ArcGIS integration for geospatial analysis
- Covers plots (not just completed attacks) -- unique differentiator
- Consistent categorization methodology
- Now owned by FiscalNote/Dow Jones, increasing distribution

**Weaknesses:**
- Smaller dataset than GTD or ACLED
- Limited real-time alerting capability
- Not a full threat intelligence platform (no dark web, no SIGINT)
- Portal UX is adequate but not cutting-edge
- Less brand recognition than Janes or SITE

**UI/UX:** Clean portal with mapping. ArcGIS integration adds geospatial power. Moderate quality.

### 1.4 Recorded Future

**What they do:** AI-powered threat intelligence platform that aggregates and analyzes data from the open web, dark web, and technical sources to provide real-time risk scoring and predictive intelligence across cyber, geopolitical, and physical security domains.

**Key features:**
- Intelligence Graph linking entities across billions of data points
- Real-time alerting and risk scoring
- Modules: Threat Intelligence, SecOps, Vulnerability, Identity, Brand, Third-Party, Geopolitical
- Insikt Group (human analyst research team)
- Integrations with SIEM, SOAR, EDR, ticketing
- Autonomous Threat Hunting (2026 launch)
- Predictive Threat Exposure Management
- API access

**Pricing:** Subscription, per-seat + modules. Small teams (5-15 analysts): mid-five to low-six figures/year. Large enterprise (20+ seats, full suite): $250,000-$500,000+/year. Acquired by Mastercard in 2024 for $2.65B.

**Target audience:** Enterprise SOC teams, CISOs, government agencies, financial services, critical infrastructure operators.

**Data sources:** 1M+ sources including open web, dark web, paste sites, code repos, technical feeds, social media, news.

**Update frequency:** Real-time/continuous.

**Strengths:**
- Most comprehensive data aggregation in the market
- Strong AI/ML for pattern detection and risk scoring
- Excellent integrations ecosystem
- Combines cyber + geopolitical + physical threat intelligence
- Massive funding/backing (Mastercard acquisition)
- Strong API for automation

**Weaknesses:**
- Primarily cyber-focused; terrorism/conflict is secondary
- Very expensive; out of reach for NGOs, academics, small govs
- Complexity: steep learning curve for non-technical users
- Geopolitical intelligence is thinner than dedicated conflict platforms
- No dedicated terrorism incident database with structured methodology
- Information overload without skilled analysts

**UI/UX:** Modern, polished SaaS interface. Dashboard-driven. High quality but complex for new users.

## 2. FREE / ACADEMIC PLATFORMS

---

### 2.1 ACLED (Armed Conflict Location & Event Data)

**What they do:** Collects, codes, and publishes real-time data on political violence and protest events worldwide, with standardized event types, actor classifications, and geographic coordinates.

**Key features:**
- Event-level coded data (battles, explosions, violence against civilians, protests, riots, strategic developments)
- ACLED Explorer: map/table/chart visualization with filters
- Conflict Index Dashboard (risk ratings)
- CAST: forecasts political violence up to 6 months ahead
- Conflict Exposure Calculator (civilian impact estimation)
- Early Warning Dashboard (merged tools, dark mode)
- API access
- Data from 1997 (Africa), expanding to global coverage

**Pricing:** Free for academic/personal non-commercial use (registration required). Commercial license required for businesses. Government/multilateral license required for public sector. Commercial pricing not publicly listed (contact access@acleddata.com).

**Target audience:** Academics, humanitarian organizations, NGOs, journalists, policymakers, commercial risk analysts (licensed).

**Data sources:** Media monitoring, local/international news, OSINT, partner organizations, field networks.

**Update frequency:** Weekly (near-real-time for some regions).

**Strengths:**
- Most comprehensive real-time conflict event dataset globally
- Standardized methodology enables cross-country comparison
- Free for academic/non-commercial use
- Growing forecasting capabilities (CAST)
- Good API for programmatic access
- Covers all forms of political violence and protests, not just terrorism

**Weaknesses:**
- Coding methodology can be inconsistent across regions/coders
- Weekly updates lag behind real-time platforms
- Commercial/government licensing costs are opaque
- Data quality varies by region (Africa strongest, newer regions weaker)
- Visualization tools are functional but basic compared to enterprise platforms
- No dark web or extremist content monitoring
- Definition of "political violence" is broad -- requires filtering for terrorism-specific analysis

**UI/UX:** Functional. Explorer is decent for basic analysis. Dashboard improved in 2025. Not as polished as enterprise tools.

---

### 2.2 UCDP (Uppsala Conflict Data Program)

**What they do:** Maintains the world's longest-running academic dataset on organized violence (1946-present), coding state-based conflicts, non-state conflicts, and one-sided violence with rigorous academic methodology.

**Key features:**
- 18 datasets with 500+ coded variables
- Interactive Conflict Encyclopedia (maps, charts, text)
- Global coverage from 1946 (some datasets from 1989)
- Encyclopedic conflict narratives
- Actor profiles, peace agreement data
- Downloadable data in multiple formats
- Charts and maps page with yearly updates

**Pricing:** Completely free. Open-access.

**Target audience:** Academic researchers, policymakers, students, peace/conflict scholars.

**Data sources:** Academic research, news monitoring, government reports, field research, systematic coding.

**Update frequency:** Annually (with some datasets updated more frequently).

**Strengths:**
- Longest time series available (1946-present)
- Rigorous academic methodology, highly cited
- Completely free and open-access
- Authoritative for academic publications
- Strong conflict definitions and coding rules
- Excellent for longitudinal analysis

**Weaknesses:**
- Annual updates make it useless for real-time monitoring
- No sub-national event-level data for most datasets
- Conservative coding: misses smaller-scale violence
- High death threshold (25 battle-related deaths/year for inclusion)
- Basic web visualization -- no modern dashboards
- No API
- No forecasting or alerting capabilities
- Academic focus means slow adaptation to new conflict types

**UI/UX:** Academic-grade. Functional but dated. Conflict Encyclopedia is informative but not visually modern.

### 2.3 Global Terrorism Database (GTD / START)

**What they do:** The most comprehensive unclassified database of terrorist attacks worldwide, maintained by the University of Maryland's START consortium, covering 200,000+ incidents from 1970 to present with 100+ coded variables per event.

**Key features:**
- 200,000+ terrorist incidents (1970-present)
- 100+ variables per event (date, location, weapons, targets, casualties, perpetrators)
- Downloadable as Excel/geodatabase
- Academic research standard for terrorism studies

**Pricing:** Free but restricted access since 2025. Requires registration and request with personal information.

**Target audience:** Academic researchers, students, policymakers, think tanks.

**Data sources:** Open-source media, existing datasets, legal proceedings, academic studies.

**Update frequency:** Annual (typically 1-2 year lag in latest data).

**Strengths:**
- Largest unclassified terrorism-specific dataset in the world
- Deep historical coverage (1970-present)
- Standard reference for academic terrorism research
- Rich variable set per incident

**Weaknesses:**
- Access restricted since 2025 (no longer freely downloadable)
- 1-2 year data lag makes it useless for real-time analysis
- No visualization platform or dashboard
- Definition of "terrorism" excludes state terrorism and some conflict violence
- Gap in data for 1993
- No forecasting or alerting
- Static dataset -- no interactive tools
- Funding/maintenance concerns long-term

**UI/UX:** Essentially a downloadable dataset. No interactive platform. Researchers must build their own analysis tools.

## 3. GOVERNMENT / PUBLIC TOOLS

---

### 3.1 DHS National Terrorism Advisory System (NTAS)

**What they do:** U.S. government system that communicates terrorism threat information to the public through bulletins and alerts, indicating whether threats are elevated or imminent.

**Key features:**
- Two advisory types: Bulletins (trends/developments) and Alerts (elevated/imminent threats)
- Geographic targeting when applicable
- Includes protective action guidance
- Alerts have expiration dates
- Embeddable widget for websites
- Replaced color-coded HSAS in 2011

**Pricing:** Free / public.

**Target audience:** General American public, state/local law enforcement, critical infrastructure operators, security professionals.

**Data sources:** U.S. Intelligence Community (classified assessments, FBI, CIA, etc.).

**Update frequency:** As needed. Bulletins issued periodically; alerts when warranted. Often months between updates.

**Strengths:**
- Authoritative (backed by full U.S. intelligence apparatus)
- Clear, actionable guidance for public
- Free and universally accessible

**Weaknesses:**
- U.S.-focused only
- Extremely vague and general (by design)
- No specific threat data, incidents, or analytics
- Very infrequent updates
- No granularity -- single national threat level
- Not useful for research or intelligence analysis
- Political considerations may influence timing/content
- Superseded by the color system but still criticized for lack of specificity

**UI/UX:** Simple government webpage. Clear but minimal.

---

### 3.2 UK MI5 Threat Level System

**What they do:** The Joint Terrorism Analysis Centre (JTAC) sets and publishes the UK national terrorism threat level on a 5-point scale, reflecting the likelihood of a terrorist attack.

**Key features:**
- 5 levels: LOW, MODERATE, SUBSTANTIAL, SEVERE, CRITICAL
- Single national level covering all forms of terrorism (Islamist, far-right, far-left, NI-related)
- Separate assessment for Northern Ireland-related terrorism in NI
- Updated as needed based on intelligence

**Pricing:** Free / public.

**Target audience:** UK public, security practitioners, police, critical infrastructure operators.

**Data sources:** UK Intelligence Community (MI5, MI6, GCHQ, police intelligence).

**Update frequency:** As needed. Can change at any time; no set schedule.

**Strengths:**
- Clear, simple communication framework
- Backed by world-class intelligence agencies
- Unified across all terrorism ideologies (since 2019)
- Actionable for security practitioners

**Weaknesses:**
- UK-only
- Single data point with no granularity
- No analysis, data, or context provided publicly
- Not useful for research
- Very high level -- same threat level can persist for months/years
- No historical data visualization or trends

**UI/UX:** Simple webpage. Single indicator. Minimal.

---

### 3.3 Global Terrorism Index (Vision of Humanity / IEP)

**What they do:** Annual index ranking 163 countries by terrorism impact (incidents, fatalities, injuries, hostages) on a 0-10 scale, produced by the Institute for Economics & Peace.

**Key features:**
- 163-country ranking
- 4 weighted indicators over 5 years
- 0-10 scale with logarithmic banding
- Interactive map
- Annual report with trend analysis and regional breakdowns
- Free downloadable PDF reports and data

**Pricing:** Free. Reports and data freely downloadable.

**Target audience:** Policymakers, media, researchers, general public, NGOs.

**Data sources:** Terrorism Tracker (Dragonfly/IEP proprietary), ACLED, government sources.

**Update frequency:** Annual (one report per year, typically March).

**Strengths:**
- Free and widely accessible
- Clean visualizations and well-written reports
- Good for high-level country comparisons
- Strong media visibility and citations
- Combines multiple indicators into digestible format

**Weaknesses:**
- Annual only -- no real-time utility
- Country-level only, no sub-national granularity
- Composite score obscures specific dynamics
- Methodology criticized for weighting choices
- Backward-looking (5-year weighted average)
- No event-level data
- No API or programmatic access
- Limited analytical depth

**UI/UX:** Clean, modern website. Good map visualization. Reports are well-designed. Moderate-high quality.

### 3.4 Fragile States Index (Fund for Peace)

**What they do:** Annual index ranking 179 countries on state fragility using 12 indicators across social, economic, political, and cohesion dimensions, drawing on content analysis of millions of documents.

**Key features:**
- 179-country ranking
- 12 indicators scored 0-10 (total 0-120 scale)
- CAST methodology (content analysis of 100,000+ sources)
- Country dashboard with historical trends
- Downloadable data
- Heat map and comparative tools

**Pricing:** Free. Open-access.

**Target audience:** Policymakers, development agencies, conflict prevention organizations, academics.

**Data sources:** Content analysis of millions of documents, quantitative data, qualitative expert review.

**Update frequency:** Annual.

**Strengths:**
- Comprehensive fragility assessment framework
- Long time series for trend analysis
- Free and open
- Combines quantitative and qualitative methods
- Well-established in policy community

**Weaknesses:**
- Not terrorism-specific (measures state fragility broadly)
- Annual only
- Country-level only
- Composite scores can be reductive
- No event-level data or real-time capability
- Methodology transparency has been questioned
- Limited predictive power for specific terrorism events

**UI/UX:** Clean website with interactive dashboard. Country profiles are useful. Moderate quality.

## 4. OPEN SOURCE / COMMUNITY

---

### 4.1 Liveuamap

**What they do:** Crowdsourced, real-time interactive map aggregating conflict events, military actions, protests, and other security-relevant incidents from social media, news, and user reports.

**Key features:**
- Real-time event mapping on interactive global map
- Dedicated regional maps (Ukraine, Israel-Palestine, Syria, etc.)
- Event filtering by type, location, time
- Social media and news source aggregation
- Mobile app (iOS, Android)
- PRO version: ad-free, satellite maps, history, search, data export

**Pricing:** Free (with ads). PRO: ~$2-5/month (ad-free + features). API available separately.

**Target audience:** General public, journalists, OSINT researchers, security professionals, military observers.

**Data sources:** Social media, news outlets, Telegram channels, user submissions, direct reports.

**Update frequency:** Real-time/continuous (crowdsourced).

**Strengths:**
- Fastest free conflict visualization tool available
- Excellent geographic coverage of active conflicts
- Extremely low barrier to entry
- Strong community engagement
- Real-time updates from social media
- Good mobile experience

**Weaknesses:**
- No quality control or verification methodology
- Social media dependency means unreliable sourcing
- No structured data or coding methodology
- Heavy Ukraine/Middle East bias in coverage
- No historical database or download capability (without PRO)
- No analytics or trend analysis
- Misinformation risk from unverified posts
- Limited coverage outside active hot conflicts
- No API for programmatic access (basic API available separately)

**UI/UX:** Excellent map interface. Clean, intuitive. One of the best UIs in this space. High quality for casual use.

### 4.2 Bellingcat

**What they do:** Investigative journalism collective specializing in OSINT investigations, fact-checking, and developing/curating open-source investigation tools and methodologies for the wider research community.

**Key features:**
- Published investigations (conflict, human rights, terrorism)
- Online Investigation Toolkit (curated, collaborative tool directory)
- Training workshops and methodology guides
- Volunteer community for tool curation
- Categories: satellite imagery, social media, transportation, archiving, geolocation

**Pricing:** Free (content and toolkit). Workshops are paid.

**Target audience:** Journalists, OSINT researchers, human rights investigators, academics, citizen investigators.

**Data sources:** Open-source investigation methods applied to satellite imagery, social media, public records, leaked data.

**Update frequency:** Investigation-by-investigation. Toolkit updated continuously by volunteers.

**Strengths:**
- Pioneered modern OSINT investigation methodology
- Free toolkit is invaluable for researchers
- High-quality, impactful investigations
- Strong training and community building
- Independent and credible
- Collaborative open-source approach

**Weaknesses:**
- Not a monitoring platform or database -- produces investigations, not data
- No real-time alerting or systematic coverage
- Investigation-driven: covers what they choose, not comprehensive
- Toolkit curates others' tools, doesn't build its own platform
- Resource-constrained as nonprofit
- Not useful for systematic terrorism monitoring or trend analysis

**UI/UX:** Clean website for articles. Toolkit (GitBook) is well-organized. Good quality for what it is.

- ShadowDragon (social media monitoring)

- Quality varies enormously across tools

---

### 4.4 ViEWS (Violence & Impacts Early-Warning System)

**What they do:** Open-source AI-based conflict forecasting system generating monthly predictions of armed conflict 1-36 months ahead at both country and sub-national levels, co-hosted by Uppsala University and PRIO.

**Key features:**
- Monthly global forecasts up to 36 months ahead
- Country-level + sub-national (0.5 degree resolution for Africa/Middle East)
- Multiple modeling approaches (neural networks, LLMs, Hidden Markov, time series)
- Open-source methodology and code
- 95% accuracy on country-months in conflict (35% false positives)
- New platform launching 2025-2026

**Pricing:** Free. Open-access. Open-source.

**Target audience:** Policymakers, humanitarian planners, conflict researchers, early warning practitioners.

**Data sources:** UCDP data, ACLED, socioeconomic indicators, satellite imagery, news feeds.

**Update frequency:** Monthly forecasts.

**Strengths:**
- Most advanced open-source conflict forecasting system
- Transparent methodology (open-source)
- Sub-national granularity for Africa/Middle East
- Strong academic backing (Uppsala + PRIO)
- Long forecast horizon (3 years)
- Free and open

**Weaknesses:**
- Sub-national coverage limited to Africa/Middle East
- Monthly updates too slow for operational response
- Forecasts are probabilistic -- not event-specific
- No real-time monitoring or alerting
- Academic tool -- not designed for operational security use
- New platform still in development
- False positive rate of 35% limits operational utility
- No terrorism-specific models (forecasts "organized violence" broadly)

**UI/UX:** Academic. New platform in development expected to improve significantly. Currently moderate quality.

## 5. GAP ANALYSIS: WHAT EVERYONE MISSES

### Pain Points Nobody Solves Well:

**1. The Real-Time + Structured Data Gap**
No platform combines real-time detection speed (Dataminr, Liveuamap) with rigorous, structured event coding (ACLED, GTD). You either get fast-but-messy or slow-but-clean. There is no "real-time ACLED."

**2. Affordable Mid-Market Pricing**
Enterprise platforms cost $100K-500K+/year. Academic tools are free but limited. There is virtually nothing in the $1,000-$10,000/year range that provides professional-grade terrorism intelligence with modern UX. The mid-market (NGOs, small security firms, journalists, think tanks) is severely underserved.

**3. Unified Terrorism + Conflict Intelligence**
No single platform combines: terrorism incident database + extremist content monitoring + conflict event data + forecasting + geospatial visualization + real-time alerts. Users must stitch together 3-5+ tools.

**4. Predictive Intelligence for Terrorism Specifically**
ViEWS forecasts "organized violence" broadly. ACLED CAST forecasts "political violence." Nobody forecasts terrorism specifically with high granularity (sub-national, by group, by tactic). Predictive models for specific terrorist attack risk remain primitive.

**5. Cross-Source Event Deduplication and Linking**
When an attack happens, ACLED codes it one way, GTD another, UCDP another, Dragonfly another. Nobody provides automated cross-referencing or deduplication across these datasets. Researchers waste enormous time reconciling sources.

**6. Non-Western Language and Source Coverage**
Most platforms are English-centric. Babel Street does 200+ languages but is expensive and government-focused. Coverage of local-language sources in Sahel, Central Asia, Southeast Asia, and Central Africa remains weak across all platforms.

**7. Modern UX for Conflict Data**
ACLED Explorer is functional but basic. UCDP is academic-grade. GTD has no platform at all. The gap between what modern SaaS dashboards look like (think Figma, Linear, Notion) and what conflict data platforms look like is enormous.

**8. Historical + Real-Time in One View**
Want to see today's attacks overlaid on 10-year trends with forecasts? Impossible without building it yourself. No platform bridges historical databases, real-time event streams, and forward-looking forecasts in a single interface.

**9. Terrorism Financing and Network Analysis**
SITE monitors extremist content. Flashpoint monitors dark web. But integrated tracking of terrorism financing flows, recruitment networks, and operational logistics across platforms is not available in any single tool. Network/graph analysis of terrorist organizations remains manual.

**10. Open API Ecosystem**
Most premium platforms are closed gardens. There is no "Stripe for terrorism data" -- a well-documented, developer-friendly API that provides structured, coded terrorism event data in real-time at reasonable cost. ACLED has an API but it's rate-limited and not real-time.

**11. Civilian Impact and Humanitarian Consequences**
Platforms track attacks but poorly track displacement, humanitarian access disruption, economic impact, and long-term community consequences of terrorism. INFORM tries at the macro level but not at event level.

**12. Plot and Pre-Attack Intelligence**
Only Dragonfly systematically tracks plots (not just completed attacks). The pre-attack planning phase -- radicalization indicators, operational planning signals, pre-operational surveillance -- is not systematically tracked by any open-source platform.
